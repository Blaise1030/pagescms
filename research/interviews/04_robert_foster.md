# Interview: Robert Foster — Retired Empty Nester

## Persona Background

Robert Foster, 64, recently retired after a career in facilities management. He and his wife Linda (62, still working part-time as a school librarian) live in a suburb of Columbus, Ohio. Their two adult children have moved out — one lives in Denver, one in Boston. Robert took over most of the cooking after retirement about two years ago, partly out of necessity and partly because he found he enjoyed it. Both he and Linda eat carefully: Robert's doctor flagged elevated LDL cholesterol at his last physical, so they've cut back on red meat and saturated fat. He owns an Amazon Echo and uses it daily for timers and weather, and recently tried ChatGPT once at his daughter's suggestion to help write a birthday card.

## Supporting Data Found

- **Cooking at home:** 62% of Americans feel confident in the kitchen (Instacart, 2025 survey); older adults 60+ are more motivated by health/nutrition than younger age groups
- **Meal patterns:** ~51.5% of adults aged 65–75 are "regular eaters"; ~33.8% are meal skippers (OUTDOOR ACTIVE Study, PMC)
- **Chronic health conditions:** Roughly 93% of older adults have at least one chronic condition; nearly 75% of adults over 60 have high blood pressure (villagecaregiving.com)
- **Cholesterol & diet:** Dietary interventions starting at retirement are effective and sustainable; increased fruit/vegetable intake by 87.5 g/day on average in intervention studies (PMC4021978)
- **Caloric needs shift:** After age 60, energy expenditure drops ~0.7% per year; men need 2,000–2,600 calories daily (seniorsite.org)
- **Technology adoption:** Smartphone ownership among adults 50+ rose from 55% (2016) to 90% (2025); adults 50+ own an average of 7 tech devices (AARP 2025)
- **Smart speaker/home assistant ownership:** 35% of adults 50+ own a home assistant (AARP 2025); ~22% of adults 55+ own a smart speaker (Statista)
- **AI usage among seniors:** AI tool usage among older adults nearly doubled from 18% (2024) to 30% (2025), driven partly by health monitoring and nutritional guidance interest (AARP 2026 report)
- **Privacy concerns:** ~70% of adults don't trust companies to use AI responsibly; older adults are the most distrustful age group regarding AI; only 27% of users understood how chatbot providers handled their data (Statista/Protecto.ai 2024)
- **Online grocery shopping:** Only 16% of Americans over 50 have ever ordered groceries for delivery; 90% of older consumers still shop at a supermarket in-person at least once a month (IFIC survey)
- **Food affordability:** More than 4 in 10 adults 50+ said grocery prices now cost more than they can afford (AARP December 2025 survey)

## Interview Transcript

### Section 1: Current Meal Planning Workflows

---

**Q1: Tell me about who you usually cook for and how your week tends to look. How predictable is your schedule? Any dietary needs that affect meals?**

Well, it's just the two of us now — me and Linda. The kids are long gone, which honestly took some adjusting to, in the kitchen especially. For thirty years I cooked for four people and suddenly it was like I didn't know how to make anything that didn't leave a week's worth of leftovers. [laughs]

Linda still works three days a week at the school library — Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday mostly — so those evenings she gets home around five-thirty and I try to have something ready or close to it. The other days we eat together at whatever pace we like. Retirement's been good for that. My schedule's pretty open. I'd say it's predictable in a loose sort of way — we have a rhythm, not a rigid timetable.

The big thing shaping what we eat is my cholesterol. Doctor told me about eighteen months ago my LDL was too high — he called it "borderline high," which I guess was meant to sound reassuring but it didn't. He said diet was the first line of defense before going on medication. So we've cut back significantly on red meat, switched to olive oil, eat more fish, more vegetables, more fiber. Linda's on board with all of it, thankfully. I've read that nearly 75% of adults over 60 have high blood pressure or some related cardiovascular issue, so I know we're not unusual. But it still changes how you think about every meal — you're reading labels, you're asking yourself "how much saturated fat is in that?" It becomes part of the habit.

We also just eat less than we used to. I've learned that your calorie needs drop as you get older — something like energy expenditure falls about 0.7% per year after 60. So what worked for me at 45 is too much now. Smaller portions, more vegetables filling the plate.

---

**Q2: Think about the last time you planned, shopped for, and cooked meals. Walk me through the whole process — planning, shopping, prep, cooking. What tools keep it together?**

Okay, so let's take last week as an example.

Sunday morning — that's my planning time. Linda and I sit with coffee and I'll look at what's in the fridge, what's running low, and we'll talk about what sounds good. I keep a small spiral notebook on the kitchen counter. Nothing fancy. I write down four or five dinners for the week. We usually don't plan Monday through Sunday exactly — more like "here are five dinners, we'll figure out the order as the week goes." Lunches we keep simple — leftovers or a sandwich, maybe soup. Breakfast is pretty routine, oatmeal or eggs.

Once I have the dinner list, I write out my grocery list on a notepad — same notepad, different page. I know some people use apps but honestly I've tried a couple and I always forget to use them or I can't figure out how to add things quickly. So paper works for me.

For shopping, I go in person to the grocery store. Almost always Kroger, Monday morning usually, when it's quieter. I like going in person because I can check what looks fresh — if the salmon looks good I might swap it in for something else I had planned. I've seen surveys saying only about 16% of people over 50 have even tried grocery delivery. That tracks with me. I tried it once during the pandemic and got substitutions I didn't want and the produce was mediocre. Haven't done it since.

For cooking, I might do a little prep Sunday afternoon — chop some vegetables, maybe cook a big pot of brown rice or grains that I can use across a few meals. But I don't do heavy meal prepping the way I see on YouTube. I prefer to cook most things the day of. It feels fresher. Linda likes it that way too.

The main tools: that spiral notebook, a few cookbooks I like — one specifically for heart-healthy cooking — and the Alexa in the kitchen for timers. I have like three timers going at once sometimes when I'm cooking a full dinner. Alexa's great for that. I'll also ask Alexa to convert measurements or remind me when something needs to come out of the oven.

---

**Q3: What's the most frustrating part of the whole cycle? Where's the biggest bottleneck? Tell me about a time something went wrong.**

Deciding what to eat is actually the hardest part, I think. Not the shopping, not the cooking — the deciding. You'd think with all this time on my hands it would be easy, but staring at five dinner options when you're also trying to balance healthy eating, not repeating the same thing too often, and using up what's already in the fridge — that's genuinely tiring some weeks. I call it "menu fatigue." Linda calls it "Robert standing in front of the open fridge for ten minutes." [laughs]

The health constraint makes it harder, not easier. It narrows the options. I can't just say "let's have burgers and fries." So I'm always mentally checking: is this too much saturated fat? Does this have enough fiber? It becomes a lot of background work.

A recent thing that went wrong — a couple months ago I planned a Thai-inspired chicken dish I'd found in a magazine. Went to the store, got everything I thought I needed. Got home, started cooking, went to grab the fish sauce — and we were completely out. I'd finished it the week before and forgotten to add it to the list. The dish doesn't work without it. I ended up improvising with soy sauce and a little lime juice, which was fine but not what I intended. Linda said it tasted good, but I was annoyed with myself.

The workaround I've tried is keeping a little running list on the fridge — a magnetic notepad — where I jot things down when I finish them or when I notice we're low. But I'm inconsistent about it. Some weeks it works, some weeks I forget.

My ideal would be something that keeps a kind of running inventory and reminds me before I go to the store — "hey, you mentioned fish sauce is running low." But that feels like something very high-tech. I don't know how you'd actually do it without scanning everything that comes in and out of the pantry.

### Section 2: Chat Behaviors & Trust

---

**Q1: When do you choose to use chat to get something done, instead of an app or a website?**

I use Alexa every day, and that's sort of chat in a way, isn't it? Voice at least. I ask her things like "how many tablespoons in a quarter cup?" or "set a timer for 20 minutes" or "what's the weather tomorrow?" Quick, factual, one-off things. That works great.

My daughter showed me ChatGPT back in — I think it was March. She had me type in "write a birthday card for someone turning 70 who loves gardening" and it spat out this perfectly nice card message in about five seconds. I was impressed. That was a task I would have sat staring at a blank page for. So I can see the use for things like that — writing help, quick questions, explaining something I don't understand.

What makes it feel faster than a website is that I don't have to sift through ten links and advertisements to find the answer. I just ask and I get something back in plain English. At my age — and I say this without embarrassment — I don't have patience for websites that make you click through three pages to find one piece of information.

What I avoid doing through chat: anything involving money, my personal accounts, health records. If someone told me I could "just chat" with my bank or my Medicare account, I'd be very wary. That's not something I want to do through a text box on the internet.

---

**Q2: What would you need to trust a chat assistant to manage something important for you? What mistakes are acceptable, and which are not?**

Trust is a big word. I've read that something like 70% of adults don't trust companies to use AI responsibly, and I'd put myself in that camp — not because I'm paranoid, but because I'm cautious. I'm from a generation where you had to earn trust. You didn't just hand someone your information on the first meeting.

To trust a chat assistant with something real — like my meal planning — I'd need to know a few things. First, where does my information go? Who sees it? Is it being used to sell me things? I read something about how most people don't even know that AI chatbots store and analyze your conversations. That bothers me. I'd want a clear, simple answer to that — not a thirty-page terms of service document.

Second, I'd need to be able to check what it did. If I told it to plan my dinners for next week, I want to see the plan before it does anything else with it — before it makes a list, before it orders anything. I want a review step. Every time.

Acceptable mistakes: it suggests a meal I don't love, or it gives me a substitution I wouldn't have picked. Fine. I'll fix it. Unacceptable mistakes: it orders something I didn't ask for, or it shares my information somewhere I didn't authorize, or it gives me health advice that conflicts with what my doctor said and I follow it without realizing. That would be a serious problem.

What would make me stop using it? If it did something I didn't tell it to do. If it felt like it was running ahead of me without my permission. I need to feel like I'm in charge. The tool should do what I ask, not what it guesses I might want.

### Section 3: Concept Reaction

---

**Q1: Imagine you could manage meals by chatting with an assistant connected to a meal planning app. What do you think it would do? What sounds useful? What sounds risky?**

Well, based on what you're describing — I imagine it's like having a very well-organized assistant that knows what you eat, what your restrictions are, and can come up with suggestions and maybe keep your grocery list. Is that roughly it?

The part that sounds most useful to me, honestly, is solving that decision fatigue problem I mentioned. If I could just say "give me five heart-healthy dinners for this week that aren't things we've had in the last two weeks" — and it actually knew what we've had recently — that would be genuinely helpful. That's the part I spend real mental energy on. I'd also love if it could remind me when something's running low in the pantry, though I'm not sure how it would know.

The part that sounds unclear or risky is the "connected" part. Connected to what? To my grocery store? To some database? To the internet? I'd want to understand exactly what it has access to and what it's doing with that access. The word "connected" is a little vague and vague tends to make me nervous.

Who is it best for? Honestly, maybe people in a situation like mine — retired, cooking for just one or two people, managing some health constraints, have the time to engage with it but could use the organizational help. I don't think it's for someone who's running around with three kids and needs food in 20 minutes. They need something faster and simpler. But for someone like me, who has the time to think about meals but gets tired of doing all the thinking — there might be something there.

---

**Q2: Using your last planning week, what would you want to do via chat, and what would you not?**

Okay. So Sunday morning planning session — instead of sitting there with the notebook and my coffee staring at nothing, I'd probably want to type something like: "Give me five low-saturated-fat dinners for this week. We had salmon last week and I'd like to use up the bunch of kale in the fridge."

I'd expect it to ask me back: Do you have any other ingredients you want to use? How long do you want to spend cooking on weeknights? Is Linda home all five nights or just some? That kind of thing. Context questions. If it just fired back five meals without asking anything, I'd feel like it wasn't really listening.

At the end I'd want a clean list — the five dinners, which nights they're planned for, and the full grocery list already sorted by section (produce, protein, pantry). That last part is something I actually do by hand now because it makes the store trip faster, and if it could do that automatically I'd appreciate it.

What would I not want to do by chat? The actual browsing of recipes in detail. I like to sit with a recipe and read it, understand the steps, maybe make notes in the margin. I don't want someone reading a recipe to me through a chat window. And I definitely wouldn't want it to automatically place a grocery order. Tell me the list, I'll shop.

Where might it break down? I go in person partly to see what looks good at the store that day. If the halibut looks terrible and the shrimp looks beautiful, I buy the shrimp. The assistant doesn't know that. So the plan is always a starting point, not a contract.

---

**Q3: What data would you be comfortable sharing, and what would be off-limits?**

Comfortable sharing: my dietary restrictions — the cholesterol thing, the need for more fiber, the foods we don't like. Our general schedule — which nights Linda's home, that sort of thing. My rough weekly food budget. Historical meal preferences — what we've enjoyed, what we haven't.

Off-limits, full stop: anything financial. Credit card information, bank account, not a chance. Health records — my actual medical file, doctor notes, prescriptions. My location beyond the basic city or zip code for things like local store availability. And I'd be very uncomfortable if I found out it was learning from my conversations to train some model or sharing my data with third parties for any reason.

I'd want to control or review before anything is saved: the meal plan itself before it's finalized, the grocery list before it's shared or acted on, and any dietary preferences it thinks it's learned about me. I'd want to be able to look at that and say "no, that's wrong, remove it."

What permissions would feel reasonable? Reading my previously entered preferences and meal history — yes. Suggesting grocery lists — yes, but not acting on them. Accessing a connected grocery account to check prices or availability — maybe, if I explicitly turned it on. Ordering anything on my behalf — no. Never without a very explicit and separate confirmation step.

What would make me feel confident? Transparency. Show me what you know about me. Let me delete it. Don't bury the data policy in fine print. If there's a simple screen that says "here's what the assistant knows about you" and I can edit or clear it — that goes a long way.

---

**Q4: What would make meal management via chat feel complete — not just a one-off suggestion?**

Good question. A one-off is easy. Anyone can give you one dinner idea. What makes something actually useful over time is that it learns without me having to keep explaining myself.

For review and editing: I'd want to be able to change the plan mid-week without starting over. Say it's Wednesday and Linda decides she doesn't want the fish dish — I should be able to say "swap Thursday's dinner for something else" and it adjusts without me re-entering everything.

For handling changes: same idea. If I find out Saturday that we're going to our son's place for Sunday dinner, I want to just say "we're eating out Sunday, adjust the plan" and it quietly removes that meal and maybe flags that some groceries I already bought won't be needed. Don't make me start from scratch.

For what I'd want it to remember over time: the things I'd have to explain to any new person I hired as a cook. We don't eat pork. I'm watching saturated fat. Linda doesn't like very spicy food. We prefer not to repeat the same protein more than twice in one week. These aren't things I want to re-enter every Sunday morning. If it remembers those things after I tell it once, that's when it starts to feel like a real tool rather than a party trick.

### Section 4: Wrap-up

---

**Q1: If you tried this, what would success look like after a week of using it?**

Success would be: Sunday morning takes me 10 minutes instead of 30. I type a few things, it gives me a sensible plan, I tweak one or two things, and I have a clean grocery list ready to go. No missing ingredients mid-week. And I get to the store with a list that's already in the right order for how I walk the aisles.

The frustration it would need to reduce most is the decision fatigue. If I never have to stare at a blank notebook again wondering "what should we eat this week," that's already a significant improvement.

A clear sign it saved me time: if Linda asks me how the planning went and I say "fine, took ten minutes" instead of making a face. [laughs] That would be a sign.

What would make me recommend it to someone? If it felt trustworthy and straightforward. Not clever, not fancy — just reliable. If I could say "I told it what we eat and what we avoid, and it's been consistently useful for four or five weeks," I'd mention it to my friend Carl. He's 67, retired, just had a cardiac event, and his wife is having him do the cooking now too. He'd benefit from the health-focused angle especially.

---

**Q2: What's the minimum it would need to do to get you to try it, and what would be a dealbreaker?**

Minimum to try it: it needs to be able to take my dietary constraints seriously from day one — not generic healthy eating advice, but specifically: low saturated fat, high fiber, heart-friendly. That has to be baked in, not an afterthought. And setup should take maybe 15 minutes, not an hour. I'm willing to put in a little time to get a useful tool, but if you're asking me to enter three years of meal history before it becomes helpful, I'll close the tab.

Dealbreaker: if it ever acted on my behalf — ordered groceries, shared data, signed me up for anything — without asking me first. Once. That would be the end. I have friends my age who've been victims of online scams and it makes everyone in this age group very cautious. The moment a tool surprises me with an action I didn't authorize, the trust is gone completely.

If my top concern — privacy and control — were genuinely addressed? If I could see clearly what it knows, easily correct it, and know it won't do anything without asking? Yes. I would try it. I'm not opposed to technology. I use Alexa every day, I'm curious about ChatGPT, and I'm open to things that genuinely make my life easier. I just need to feel like I'm the one driving.

## Key Insights Summary

- **Decision fatigue is the core pain point.** Robert spends the most mental energy on what to cook rather than the cooking itself. Health constraints (cholesterol management) narrow his options and amplify this weekly friction — a chat assistant that intelligently narrows choices based on durable preferences would address his primary frustration.
- **Trust and control are non-negotiable preconditions for adoption.** Robert falls squarely within the majority of older adults who distrust AI data handling. He will not engage with a tool that acts autonomously or obscures what it knows. Explicit review steps, data transparency, and zero unauthorized actions are table stakes — not enhancements.
- **In-person grocery shopping is load-bearing, not just habitual.** Robert's store trip involves real-time quality decisions (choosing protein based on what looks fresh) that no automated ordering system can replicate. The right role for chat is producing an organized, sortable list — not fulfilling the order.
- **Persistence and memory dramatically increase perceived value.** A one-shot suggestion is mildly interesting; a tool that remembers "no pork, low saturated fat, Linda dislikes heat, no protein repeated twice" without being re-taught each week is genuinely useful. The transition from novelty to utility depends entirely on durable personalization.
- **The referral vector is peer health.** Robert would recommend the tool to a recently retired friend managing a cardiac condition — not a younger, busier person. The product's word-of-mouth pathway among this demographic runs through shared health concerns, not convenience or novelty.
