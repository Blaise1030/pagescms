# Interview: Sarah Chen — Busy Working Mom

## Persona Background

Sarah Chen, 38, works full-time as a marketing manager in a suburban household. She is married with two kids — Maya, 8, and Ethan, 11. Her husband David is actively trying to eat low-carb. Maya is a picky eater with strong food preferences. Sarah is the primary meal planner in the household, handling the full cycle from weekly planning through grocery shopping and cooking most nights. She uses a mix of digital tools and mental heuristics to stay on top of meals.

- **Consumer trust in AI:** 79% of Americans still prefer interacting with a human over an AI agent for important decisions; trust is highly context-dependent (Pew Research, 2026).

## Interview Transcript

### Section 1: Current Meal Planning Workflows

---

**Q1: Tell me about who you usually cook for and how your week tends to look. How predictable is your schedule week to week? Any dietary needs or strong preferences that affect meals?**

Okay, so — cooking for four. Me, my husband David, and our two kids Maya and Ethan. Maya is eight and honestly she is the most stressful part of any meal plan. She has a list of foods she will eat that feels like it gets shorter every few months. Right now it's basically pasta, chicken nuggets, quesadillas, and anything that doesn't touch anything else on the plate. Ethan is fine, he eats almost everything, which is a relief. And David started doing low-carb about eight months ago — he's lost weight with it and feels better, so I completely support it, but it genuinely doubles the planning complexity for me.

As for schedule — I wouldn't call it "predictable." David has late client calls on Tuesdays. One or both kids usually have something after school two or three nights a week. I work until five most days, but it's not uncommon for something to blow up at work and suddenly I'm not home until six-thirty with no plan and a hungry eight-year-old screaming at me from the kitchen. I cook most nights. I've read that 80% of women are primarily responsible for meal planning in their households — I believe every single percent of that.

---

**Q2: Think about the last time you planned, shopped for, and cooked meals. Walk me through that entire process step-by-step. What tools, apps, or physical methods keep this entire process together?**

So Sunday is "planning day," although that makes it sound more organized than it is. Usually what happens is: Saturday night I'm lying in bed trying to remember if we have chicken in the freezer. Then Sunday morning I open our family calendar on my phone, see what's happening that week, and mentally try to match "quick meals" to the busy nights.

I use a combination of things. I keep a running grocery list in the Notes app on my iPhone — very glamorous, I know. For recipes I bounce between a couple of websites I've bookmarked and sometimes just Pinterest. I don't use a dedicated meal planning app. I tried one a few years ago but it was too rigid. I need flexibility, not a color-coded schedule.

Once I have a rough plan — usually five dinners with two buffer nights where we order out or do leftovers — I build the grocery list in Notes. I organize it by store section loosely in my head. Then I either go in-person to Kroger on Sunday afternoon or I do a pickup order. I've been doing pickup more lately. Data shows that about 55% of American adults bought groceries online in 2024, and I'm firmly in that group now. It saves time. I don't wander.

Meal prepping I try to do on Sunday too — usually it's just chopping vegetables, marinating something, and cooking a big batch of rice or grain that can be repurposed. David's meals often need to be carb-free versions of what the rest of us eat, so I'll make something like taco bowls where I prep everything separately and he just skips the tortilla and rice. Maya gets a separate little safe-food component. It works, but honestly building one dinner that actually hits four different configurations is exhausting.

---

**Q3: What is the most frustrating part of managing the entire cycle — from planning to grocery shopping, prepping, and cooking?**

The decision-making. Hands down. It's not the actual cooking, I actually like cooking when I'm not under time pressure. It's the mental load of deciding what to make, making sure it works for David's low-carb thing, making sure Maya will eat something on the plate, making sure I'm not buying ingredients that will rot because I only need two tablespoons of something for one recipe.

The food waste part really stings me. We throw away more than I want to admit. The EPA puts average food waste for a family of four at around $2,900 a year — close to $56 per week. I believe that number. For us it shows up in the form of a bag of spinach that went slimy, half a bunch of cilantro, the rest of a block of cheese nobody finished. It adds up.

The most recent thing that went wrong: two weeks ago I planned a sheet pan chicken and vegetable dinner. I had the recipe open, I thought I had everything. Got home Tuesday after a long day, started cooking — no lemon. The whole dish is built around the lemon. And David texted to say he'd be thirty minutes late. So I'm improvising with lime and apple cider vinegar, Maya is announcing she doesn't like "that smell," and Ethan is fine but somehow deeply unhelpful. The dinner was okay in the end but I was genuinely frazzled for the rest of the night.

What I do to work around it is I've started keeping a very tight "pantry staples" checklist — olive oil, canned tomatoes, pasta for Maya, frozen chicken breasts — that I never let run out. It gives me an escape hatch. My ideal workflow would be something that already knows what I have, knows what everyone will eat, and just... tells me the five dinners for the week without me having to think it up. And then builds the grocery list. Without me having to cross-reference two apps and a calendar.

### Section 2: Chat Behaviors and Trust

---

**Q1: When do you choose to use chat to get something done, instead of an app or website? What's a recent task you did through chat? What makes chat feel faster or easier in that moment?**

I've started using ChatGPT probably four or five times a week. Not for anything deeply personal, but for work stuff — drafting emails, summarizing documents, brainstorming campaign angles. And honestly, a few times for meals. Last week I typed something like "give me three low-carb dinners my picky eight-year-old might also eat a component of" and it was genuinely faster than opening Pinterest and scrolling for thirty minutes.

Chat feels faster when I know roughly what I want but I can't be bothered to navigate an interface. Like, I don't want to click through filters on a recipe site — I just want to say what I need in plain language and get an answer. The friction is lower. I'm already texting and messaging all day; chat is a familiar modality.

What I avoid doing through chat: anything that requires precise action in the world. I wouldn't trust a chat assistant to, say, actually place a grocery order on my behalf without me reviewing it first. And I don't use it for things where the stakes are high and I can't easily verify the output. I've read that about 85% of people who use AI assistants double-check the answers somewhere else — that matches my behavior exactly.

---

**Q2: What would you need to trust a chat assistant to manage something important for you? How do you check whether it did the right thing? What mistakes are acceptable, and which are not?**

Trust is earned incrementally for me. I'd need to see it get things right consistently for low-stakes stuff before I'd let it touch anything that affects my family's food or budget. 

The way I check it: I read the output. I don't just accept it and move on. If it gives me a weekly meal plan, I'm going to look at every meal and ask myself "would Maya actually eat anything here?" and "is this actually low-carb?" I've seen AI confidently say a dish is low-carb when it absolutely is not.

Acceptable mistakes: suggesting a recipe I end up not liking, giving me a slightly off grocery quantity. Those are recoverable. Not acceptable: putting something on my grocery list that we're allergic to — thankfully no serious allergies in our house, but I think about this — or ordering something on my behalf without confirmation. Or giving me confidently wrong nutritional information that David is relying on for his health goals.

What would make me stop using it: if it felt like it wasn't listening to my constraints. If I told it Maya doesn't eat mushrooms and three weeks later it's still suggesting mushroom risotto. Or if it made me feel like I was doing more work to manage the assistant than I was saving. That's the trap with a lot of apps — they promise to reduce overhead but they add a new layer of overhead.

### Section 3: Concept Reaction

---

**Q1: Imagine you could manage meals by chatting with an assistant connected to a meal planning app. What do you think it would do? What part sounds most useful, and what sounds unclear or risky?**

My first instinct is: I hope it can hold context. Like, not just answer a one-off question but actually know that David is low-carb, Maya won't touch mushrooms or anything "mixed together," and that we tend to do something easy on Tuesdays because of his schedule. If it's connected to an actual planning app, I assume it could remember that stuff between conversations, not make me re-explain my family every single time.

The most useful thing: if I could just say "plan this week" and it uses that stored context to generate a real plan, including a grocery list organized by category, maybe cross-referenced with what I have in my pantry if I've told it that. That would save me probably forty-five minutes every Sunday.

What sounds unclear or risky: anything involving actually executing something — ordering groceries, modifying subscriptions, sending things. I'd want to review and confirm before anything leaves my hands. There's also the question of where my data goes. I'd be hesitant if I didn't understand how my family's dietary info and preferences were being stored or used.

Who I think this is best for: honestly, people like me. Dual-income households, multiple dietary constraints, people who are comfortable with technology but time-starved. I don't think it's for someone who loves to browse recipes and sees that as enjoyable. That's not where I am right now.

---

**Q2: Using your last planning week, what would you want to do via chat, and what would you not?**

Via chat: "Plan five dinners for this week. David is low-carb. Maya is picky — safe foods are pasta, chicken, quesadillas, and plain rice. We have a late night Tuesday. Use what's in the pantry if you can — I have chicken breasts, canned tomatoes, pasta, and rice." That is what I'd actually type. Then I'd want it to come back with a plan I can scan in thirty seconds.

After that, I'd want it to say "here's a grocery list — does anything need to change before I save it?" and let me make edits.

What I would NOT do via chat: I wouldn't want to execute the grocery order through chat. Too many things can go wrong. I'd want to look at the order in the actual grocery app or website where I can see prices, check substitutions, confirm quantities. The chat is the planning layer; the actual transaction stays in the familiar interface.

Where it might break down in my real week: mid-week pivot. If I planned chicken on Thursday but we end up eating out Wednesday unexpectedly and have leftovers, I need to be able to text the assistant "we have leftover pasta, skip Thursday's meal, push it to Friday" and have it actually update the plan and adjust the remaining grocery items. If that's not possible, the plan is basically useless after Tuesday.

---

**Q3: What data would you be comfortable sharing for this to work, and what would be off-limits?**

Comfortable sharing: dietary preferences, food restrictions, typical schedule structure ("busy Tuesdays"), rough pantry inventory, past meals I've liked or disliked, number of people in the household, and rough budget range.

Off-limits: payment information it can act on autonomously, detailed health data beyond basic dietary goals, anything that links to my kids' identities specifically. I wouldn't want "Maya" stored as a data point tied to a real child. I'd frame it as "kid 1, age 8, picky eater" in a preference profile.

What I'd want to control before anything is saved: I'd want to review any saved preference profile before it's stored. Let me see what you think you know about me and edit it. Same for any plan before it's "committed" to the app. A confirm step is not optional for me.

Permissions that feel reasonable: reading my calendar to understand my schedule, reading from a pantry list I've actively maintained, saving meal plans I've approved. Permissions that feel like too much: accessing my contacts, reading my emails, any push into external services without an explicit "go ahead" from me each time.

What would make me feel confident it's using data appropriately: a clear, plain-language summary of what it stores and what it does with it. Not a terms-of-service wall — an actual human-readable explanation. And the ability to delete everything at any time.

---

**Q4: What would make meal management via chat feel complete, not just a one-off suggestion?**

It needs to be stateful across a whole week and across weeks. If I have to start from scratch every Sunday, it's just a fancier search engine. What makes it feel complete is if it remembers that we hated that recipe two weeks ago, notices that I keep skipping the fish meal, and eventually stops suggesting fish.

I'd need to be able to review and edit the plan. Not just see it — actually adjust it. "Move Tuesday's meal to Thursday." "Swap the salmon for chicken." That has to be conversational and fast, not a form I fill out.

Mid-week handling is critical. Life changes. I need to be able to say "we're not cooking tonight, we ordered pizza" and have the assistant acknowledge that and adjust the rest of the week's plan accordingly — including updating the grocery list if something hasn't been bought yet.

Over time, I'd want it to build a recipe library I've implicitly curated just by using it. The meals I've approved, the ones I've skipped, the ones the kids ate without complaint — that's valuable signal. After three months of using this, it should know my family better than I could explain in a single setup session.

---

### Section 4: Wrap-up

---

**Q1: If you tried this, what would success look like after a week of using it?**

Success after one week: I spent less than fifteen minutes on Sunday planning. That's the number. Right now it takes me forty-five minutes minimum — looking at the calendar, thinking up meals, building the grocery list, cross-checking what I have. If this cuts that to fifteen, including review time, I'm a convert.

The frustrations it needs to reduce most: the decision fatigue around "what do I make that works for four different sets of constraints," and the food waste. If the grocery list is tighter because it's based on a real plan with real quantities, and I'm not buying a bunch of cilantro for one recipe, that's a tangible win.

A clear sign it saved me time: if David asks "what are we having this week?" on Sunday and I can answer him without opening four different tabs. That sounds small but it would feel enormous.

What would make me recommend it: if it got through a full week without me having to correct something embarrassing — like suggesting a meal Maya obviously wouldn't touch, or missing that David's dish needed to be low-carb. One week of that and I'd be texting it to my friends.

---

**Q2: What's the minimum it would need to do for you to try it, and what would be a dealbreaker?**

Minimum to try it: let me input my family's constraints once, generate a weekly meal plan with a grocery list, and let me edit both conversationally. That's the core. If it does those three things reasonably well, I'll use it.

Setup effort that feels worth it: thirty minutes max to configure my family profile, dietary constraints, and pantry staples. Beyond that it starts to feel like homework.

Dealbreakers: if it requires me to re-enter my family's constraints every session, that's dead on arrival. If it ever confidently suggests something that violates a constraint I've clearly stated — Maya hates mushrooms and it puts mushrooms in the plan — I would lose trust immediately and probably not come back. And if it touches my grocery order or any external service without explicit per-action confirmation from me, I'm done.

If my top concern — the mid-week adaptability — were addressed? Yes, I'd try it. That's the thing that breaks every meal planning system I've ever used. They're great for Sunday but they fall apart by Wednesday. If this one could actually flex with me in real time, that's the thing that would make it genuinely different.

## Key Insights Summary

- **The real bottleneck is decision fatigue, not cooking.** Sarah enjoys cooking but is exhausted by the upstream work: generating meal ideas that satisfy four people with divergent constraints simultaneously. Any solution that removes the "thinking up what to make" step has immediate value.
- **Multi-constraint households are underserved.** Managing a low-carb adult and a picky child in the same kitchen doubles planning complexity. Sarah has improvised workarounds (component-style meals, pantry staples backups) that a well-designed assistant could formalize and automate.
- **Trust is earned incrementally and context-specific.** Sarah uses AI regularly for work but maintains a clear boundary: she will plan via chat but not execute transactions via chat. Any feature that crosses into autonomous action — placing orders, making changes without review — will trigger distrust.
- **Mid-week adaptability is the feature gap that kills existing tools.** Static weekly plans break down quickly in a real household. The ability to update a plan conversationally mid-week ("we ordered pizza, adjust the rest") is the single strongest differentiator Sarah articulates.
- **Data comfort is conditional and profile-level.** Sarah is willing to share significant preference data if she can see it, edit it, and delete it. She draws a hard line at payment autonomy and anything that exposes her children's identities. Transparency and a plain-language data policy are prerequisites for trust, not nice-to-haves.
