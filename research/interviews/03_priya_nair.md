**Name:** Priya Nair

## Interview Transcript

### Section 1: Current Meal Planning Workflows

---

**Q1: Tell me about who you usually cook for and how your week tends to look. How predictable is your schedule week to week? Any dietary needs or strong preferences that affect meals?**

So it's me and my partner, Dev. He eats pretty much whatever I make, which honestly makes my life easier — I don't have to cook two different things. The challenge is his macros don't really matter to him, but mine absolutely do, so I'm always calibrating around my targets and then his portions are just... more generous. He's not tracking anything. I am tracking everything.

My schedule is the biggest wildcard. I'm a nurse, so I do rotating shifts — 12-hour days, sometimes nights. One week I might work Monday, Tuesday, Friday. The next it's Sunday, Wednesday, Thursday. I never really have the same week twice. That's actually why meal prep became non-negotiable for me. If I don't prep on Sunday, I genuinely have no idea what I'm eating Tuesday after a night shift. I'll just grab whatever's in the hospital canteen, and that's usually not hitting 160 grams of protein. There's research on this — a study I read found 65% of nurses consume less protein and fewer vegetables during work shifts just because of time constraints. I believe it.

Dietary needs-wise: I'm on a high-protein diet for powerlifting performance. I track macros religiously through MyFitnessPal. My targets are roughly 160g protein, moderate carbs, lower fat. I don't have any allergies or restrictions. I just need protein at every single meal and I need to know the numbers.

---

**Q2: Think about the last time you planned, shopped for, and cooked meals. Walk me through that entire process step-by-step.**

Okay, so Saturday night — it's almost always Saturday night after the gym — I sit down and I figure out what I'm making for the week. I open MyFitnessPal first and look at my macro targets. Then I open Instagram or just... my Notes app where I've saved recipes, and I try to pick proteins. I usually anchor the week around three or four proteins: chicken breast, ground turkey, maybe salmon, eggs. Then I build meals around those.

Then I build a shopping list. I do this manually in the Notes app — just bullet points. I've tried dedicated list apps, but I always come back to Notes. It's faster for me.

Sunday morning I go to the grocery store. I prefer in-store over delivery because I like to check the meat — I want to see what looks good, what's on sale. I'll spend around $80 to $100 a week on groceries for the two of us. A lot of that is protein — chicken thighs in bulk, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, eggs by the dozen. For fitness-focused meal prep, studies show you can actually build balanced high-protein meals for under $3 a serving if you buy in bulk, and that's roughly what I'm doing.

Then Sunday afternoon is the actual prep. It's usually 2.5 to 3 hours. I'm roasting two trays of chicken, cooking a big pot of rice, maybe making a turkey mince sauce, boiling eggs, portioning out snacks. Everything goes into containers labelled by day. Dev gets his portions separately.

I use MyFitnessPal to log everything as I prep. I'll weigh ingredients with a kitchen scale, enter them, and then divide by portions. It's precise, but it's genuinely time-consuming. The app, the scale, the Notes list — it's like three tools doing the job of something that should probably be one.

---

**Q3: What is the most frustrating part of managing the entire cycle?**

**Where is the biggest bottleneck or source of stress?**

Honestly? Variety. Or the complete lack of it. The planning phase is where I feel the most mental drain. Not the actual cooking — I can cook on autopilot at this point — but deciding *what* to cook. Every Saturday night I'm staring at the same apps thinking, "I cannot eat chicken and rice again." But I also know that chicken and rice hits my macros and I'm tired and I don't have time to experiment.

There's actually a name for what I experience — macro tracking burnout. Nutritionists have written about it: when tracking becomes so mentally taxing that it starts working against you. I'm not at the point of quitting, but I understand why people do. And the variety thing compounds it. I want interesting food, but interesting food is harder to log accurately, harder to prep in bulk, and more likely to blow my macros.

**Tell me about a recent time something went wrong.**

Two weeks ago I forgot to defrost the salmon I'd bought. Found it still solid in the freezer on Wednesday night after a 12-hour shift, absolutely not in the mood to problem-solve. I ended up eating a protein shake and leftover rice for dinner. Dev ordered pizza. I was annoyed — not at him, just at the whole situation. I'd planned, I'd shopped, I'd prepped, and one small logistical failure wrecked a meal.

**How do you work around these frustrations today?**

I rotate the same four or five weekly templates. Literally the same weeks cycling every month. It reduces the decision fatigue but I am so bored of my own food. An ideal workflow? I want something that knows my macro targets, generates a weekly plan I haven't made before, checks what I already have, builds my shopping list, and just... does that for me. The planning and the logistics. Not the cooking — I like cooking. I just hate the Saturday night planning session.

### Section 2: Chat Behaviors and Trust

---

**Q1: When do you choose to use chat to get something done, instead of an app or website?**

**What's a recent task you did through chat?**

I use it when I need a fast answer that would take me three tabs to find. Last week I asked ChatGPT to give me a high-protein lunch idea under 600 calories with what I had in the fridge — I typed out what I had and it gave me a recipe in about 10 seconds. That would've taken me 10 minutes on Google or Pinterest. Or it would've been another round of the same chicken wrap I've been eating since January.

**What makes chat feel faster or easier?**

The back-and-forth. I can give it context — "I have chicken, I already had rice twice this week, I want something under 500 calories" — and it responds to all of that at once. A search engine doesn't do that. An app with filters doesn't do that. Chat feels like talking to a person who just happens to know a lot about nutrition.

**What kinds of tasks do you avoid doing through chat?**

Anything where I need to *trust* an output but can't verify it easily. Like, I wouldn't ask a chat assistant to calculate my macros for a whole week and just take those numbers as gospel. I'd still want to verify them in MyFitnessPal. And I wouldn't use chat for anything involving my finances or personal accounts — not because I think it's necessarily unsafe, but I haven't built that trust yet.

---

**Q2: What would you need to trust a chat assistant to manage something important for you?**

**How do you check whether it did the right thing?**

I'd need it to show its work. Like, if it tells me a meal is 420 calories and 38g protein, I want to know *which* ingredients it used and at *what* quantity. I'll spot-check in MyFitnessPal. If it's consistently right — within a 5% margin let's say — I'll start trusting the numbers more over time.

**What mistakes are acceptable, and which are not?**

A recipe suggestion that doesn't quite fit my macros — fine, I can adjust. If it generates a meal plan and forgets I don't eat shellfish — I don't actually have that restriction, but hypothetically — that's annoying but fixable. What would genuinely bother me is if it placed a grocery order based on wrong quantities, or if it logged food I hadn't actually eaten into my tracker. That would break trust immediately. The stakes with nutrition tracking are real. I'm tracking for performance, not just vaguely trying to eat healthy.

**What would make you stop using it?**

If it kept making the same type of mistake after I corrected it once. Or if it felt like it was generating generic answers rather than answers for *me*. If I've told it I target 160g protein and it keeps giving me 120g plans, I'm done. Also: if I find out it's sharing my food log or health data with third parties without my explicit knowledge.

### Section 3: Concept Reaction

---

**Q1: Imagine you could manage meals by chatting with an assistant connected to a meal planning app. What do you think it would do?**

**What part sounds most useful to you, and why?**

In my head, it would basically be a very smart meal planning brain that knows my profile — my macros, my schedule, what I prepped last week — and generates a fresh weekly plan that I don't have to think about. And then it builds the shopping list automatically. That Saturday night planning session is genuinely the part I dread most. If a chat assistant could take that over, that's the time and mental energy I want back.

I'm also intrigued by the idea of it knowing what's in my pantry. Like, "Hey, you still have two portions of turkey mince and a bag of lentils from last week — here's how to use those before they go off." That would reduce food waste, which I'm bad at.

**What part sounds unclear or risky?**

I'd worry about it not understanding the precision I need. Most nutrition apps are built for general wellness users — people trying to lose a bit of weight. I'm optimizing for athletic performance. The margin for error in my macro targets is tight. A chat assistant that treats all high-protein diets the same would be frustrating fast.

Also: if it's connected to a grocery delivery service and can actually place orders — who's reviewing that? What if it orders two kilos of salmon because it made a batch size error? I need a confirmation step before anything gets ordered.

**Who do you think this would be best for?**

Honestly, people exactly like me — structured, already tracking macros, already meal prepping, but exhausted by the planning overhead. People who have the system but need the cognitive load reduced. I don't think it would work well for someone who's brand new to nutrition tracking, because you'd need to know enough to catch errors. The AI nutrition market grew from $1.6 billion in 2022 to $3.7 billion in 2024 — that tells me a lot of people are trying this, but "trying" and "trusting" are different things.

---

**Q2: Using your last planning week, what would you want to do via chat, and what would you not?**

**What would you type or say first?**

Probably something like: "Plan my meals for next week. My targets are 160g protein, 200g carbs, 70g fat per day. I'm cooking Sunday. I've already had chicken four times this week so avoid that. I'm working nights Tuesday and Wednesday so those meals need to be easy to pack."

**What would you expect the assistant to ask you back?**

I'd want it to ask: Do I have any ingredients at home already? Any meals I want to repeat that I've enjoyed before? Budget range for groceries? How many servings am I prepping — just me or Dev too? If it just generated a plan without asking those things, I'd feel like it was giving me a template, not a real plan.

**What output would you want at the end?**

A complete meal plan by day, macros listed per meal and totalled per day, and a consolidated shopping list grouped by category — produce, proteins, dairy, pantry. Ideally exportable to my Notes app or directly to a delivery cart.

**Where might this break down in your real week?**

Mid-week, something always changes. I get called in for an extra shift, Dev decides he's going to a friend's dinner and suddenly I'm cooking for one for two nights. Or I'm just too tired on Thursday after a night shift and I need something that's ready in five minutes, not the elaborate stuffed peppers I prepped. If the assistant can't adapt to mid-week changes conversationally — like, "Thursday's too much, give me something simpler" — it'll feel like a static plan, not a live assistant.

---

**Q3: What data would you be comfortable sharing for this to work, and what would be off-limits?**

**What data would you share?**

My macro targets, dietary preferences, schedule patterns (which days I work), what I've prepped before, my approximate grocery budget, proteins I like and dislike, and cooking skill level. That's all fair game — it's the information I'd give a nutritionist.

**What would be off-limits?**

My location data — I don't need a meal planning app to know where I am. My MyFitnessPal login credentials — I'd want a proper integration with permissions, not me handing over a password. Anything related to my health records. I'm a nurse; I'm very aware of how sensitive health data is. Also, I wouldn't want it building a profile on me that gets sold to food brands. A study I came across found two-thirds of consumers consider it "very" or "extremely" important that companies disclose when AI is used in food decisions — I'm firmly in that two-thirds.

**What would you want to control or review before anything is saved?**

Every meal plan before it's finalized. Every shopping list before anything is ordered. I want an explicit "confirm" step. I also want to be able to edit line items — if it's suggesting organic salmon and I want to sub in frozen salmon fillets for budget reasons, I need to be able to change that easily without starting the whole conversation over.

**What permissions would feel reasonable vs too much?**

Reasonable: reading my macro goals and past meal history from MyFitnessPal, reading my stated preferences. Too much: writing back to my MyFitnessPal log without my explicit confirmation per entry, accessing my payment methods directly, or sharing any of my data with third parties.

---

**Q4: What would make meal management via chat feel complete, not just a one-off suggestion?**

**What would you need to review, edit, or undo?**

I'd want a weekly summary I can review before Sunday prep starts — something like a "here's your plan, here's your list, ready to lock in?" moment. And I'd want an undo if I realize on Saturday afternoon that I've already got three bags of lentils and the plan is calling for me to buy more.

**How would you want it to handle changes mid-week?**

This is actually where the chat format really shines over an app. On Thursday at 9 PM after a night shift I don't want to navigate a dashboard. I want to type "I'm exhausted and I don't want the stuffed peppers" and have it say "okay, here's a 10-minute version using what you prepped Sunday." That real-time adaptability is the point.

**What would you want it to remember over time?**

That I like spicy food. That I'm bored of chicken. That I'm always short on time on nights. That I had a bad experience with a certain recipe (the salmon patties that fell apart — never again). Over time it should be building a profile of my actual preferences, not just my stated targets. The difference between a tool I use once and a tool I rely on is whether it gets smarter about me over time.

### Section 4: Wrap-up — Priorities and Adoption Thresholds

---

**Q1: If you tried this, what would success look like after a week of using it?**

**Which earlier frustrations would it need to reduce the most?**

The Saturday night planning session. If I get to Saturday and I don't have to spend 45 minutes staring at recipe apps trying to calculate whether a new meal fits my macros — that's success. That's the single biggest time and mental energy drain in my week. According to what I've read, the typical Sunday meal prepper invests 2–3 hours in actual cooking, but the planning overhead on top of that is the part nobody talks about.

**What would be a clear sign it saved you time or stress?**

If I actually have variety in my meals — if week 3 doesn't look like week 1 — I'd know it's working. And if I stop dreading the planning phase. That's a low bar, maybe, but it's a real one.

**What would make you recommend it to someone?**

If the macro accuracy is solid and consistent. If I could tell a friend "I've checked it for four weeks and it's always within 5% of what MyFitnessPal shows," that's a recommendation. I'd also recommend it if the chat actually feels responsive to my context rather than generic. Nurses talk to nurses. If it worked for me, I'd tell every colleague who complains about eating badly on shift.

---

**Q2: What's the minimum it would need to do for you to try it, and what would be a dealbreaker?**

**What setup effort would feel worth it?**

I'd spend 15–20 minutes on setup — entering my macro targets, dietary preferences, schedule patterns, foods I like and dislike. That's comparable to setting up a new fitness app. I've done it with MyFitnessPal, I've done it with others. 20 minutes is reasonable.

What I wouldn't do: manually enter a week's worth of past meals to "train" it. I'd expect it to pull that from MyFitnessPal via an integration if it needs historical data.

**What would be a dealbreaker?**

If it gets the macros consistently wrong. If it's not transparent about where its numbers come from. If it feels like a chatbot giving me generic recipes rather than a system that actually knows my context. And — this is a hard line — if I ever found out it logged food into my tracker without my confirmation, or shared my health data in a way I hadn't consented to. I work in healthcare. I know what data misuse looks like and I will not tolerate it from a meal planning app.

**If your top concern were addressed, would you try it?**

Yes. If the macro accuracy is verifiable and the privacy controls are explicit, I'm in. The planning fatigue is real enough that I'd genuinely welcome help. I just need to trust it first — and trust for me is built slowly, through consistent accuracy, not through a feature list.

## Key Insights Summary

- **Planning fatigue, not cooking, is the core pain point.** Priya has the cooking and tracking dialled in — her bottleneck is the Saturday night decision-making overhead before prep even begins. Any tool that reduces planning cognitive load is addressing the right problem.

- **Precision is non-negotiable.** Unlike casual meal planners, Priya tracks to athletic performance targets (160g protein/day). Generic suggestions or even 10–15% macro inaccuracies are not acceptable. The tool must earn trust through verifiable, consistent accuracy.

- **Shift-work unpredictability demands mid-week adaptability.** Static weekly plans break by Tuesday. The chat format is particularly well-suited here — Priya described exactly how she'd want to use conversational adjustment after a night shift, and no traditional app UI matches that use case.

- **Trust is conditional and privacy-gated.** Priya will share preferences and targets freely, but draws a hard line at automated actions (placing orders, logging food) without explicit confirmation, and at any data sharing without informed consent. Confirmation steps and permission transparency are adoption prerequisites, not nice-to-haves.

- **Variety is an underserved need in macro-tracked meal prep.** Priya rotates the same 4–5 weekly templates out of exhaustion, despite wanting diversity. A system that generates novel macro-compliant plans from her existing preference history would directly address a gap that MyFitnessPal and her current workflow cannot fill.
