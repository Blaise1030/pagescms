# Interview: Marcus Williams — Young Urban Professional

## Persona Background

Marcus Williams, 27, is a software developer living alone in a one-bedroom apartment in a mid-sized US city. He works hybrid — three days in the office, two at home — with unpredictable sprint schedules that frequently bleed into evenings. He is health-conscious in principle but inconsistent in practice: he tracks his steps, reads nutrition labels, and feels genuine guilt when he orders delivery three nights in a row, yet that happens more than he'd like. He uses Claude and ChatGPT daily for work tasks (code review, documentation, explaining error messages) and considers AI tools a natural part of his workflow. He has tried meal prep exactly twice — once in college and once during a New Year's resolution — and abandoned both attempts within two weeks.

## Supporting Data Found

- **62% of working professionals** report limited time for cooking due to work schedules exceeding 8 hours daily (Meal Prep Market Industry Report, MarketReportsWorld 2025).
- **Millennials cook at home an average of 6.7 days a week** — more than Gen Z — but **50% rely on meal prep kits or ready-to-eat meals weekly**, the highest of any generation (HelloFresh State of Home Cooking Report 2025–2026).
- **The average American spends ~37 minutes per day** on food prep, serving, and cleanup combined (BLS American Time Use Survey 2024).
- **Nearly 63% of Gen Z** regularly use food delivery apps; **~40% of people aged 29–44** use a food delivery app at least once a week (YouGov 2024/2025).
- **The average Gen Z/millennial household spends ~$210/month on food delivery** (The Globe and Mail, citing 2025 data).
- **About two-thirds of Gen Z and millennials** say ordering takeout is "an essential part of their lifestyle" (Deliverect/GoKnit 2024–2025).
- **61% of US households bought groceries online** by year-end 2025; grocery delivery usage rose 56% vs. 2022 (CapitalOneShopping Research / LocalExpress 2025).
- **84% of developers use or plan to use AI tools**; **51% use them daily** (Stack Overflow Developer Survey 2025).
- **ChatGPT (82%) and GitHub Copilot (68%)** are the most-used AI tools among developers (Second Talent / GetPanto 2025).
- **AI tools save developers ~3.6 hours per week** on average (DX AI-Assisted Engineering Q4 2025 Report).
- **US households waste ~$728 per person per year** in food, or about 6.2 cups of edible food per week (EPA / RTS 2024–2025).
- **Positive developer sentiment toward AI tools dropped** from 70%+ (2023–2024) to 60% in 2025 — mostly driven by accuracy concerns (Stack Overflow Survey 2025).

### Section 1: Current Meal Planning Workflows

---

**Q1: Tell me about who you usually cook for and how your week tends to look. How predictable is your schedule week to week? Any dietary needs or strong preferences?**

**Marcus:** It's just me. Single, no roommates, so I'm cooking for one — which sounds easier than it is, honestly, because every recipe is built for four people and I either eat the same thing five days straight or half of it goes bad. My schedule is all over the place. I'm a developer, so when we're in a sprint or there's a production issue, 6pm turns into 9pm instantly. I probably have two or three reliable evenings a week and the rest are unpredictable.

Diet-wise, I'm not strict about anything. I try to eat enough protein — I lift a few times a week — and I'm loosely trying to avoid ultra-processed stuff, but I'm not like, tracking macros obsessively. I'm more in the category of "health-aware but easily derailed." I don't have allergies or anything that would complicate things.

The stat I saw somewhere that stuck with me was that the average American spends 37 minutes a day on food prep and cleanup combined. I genuinely think I'm below that. On a good week, maybe 20 minutes. On a bad week, I'm just opening DoorDash.

---

**Q2: Think about the last time you planned, shopped for, and cooked meals. Walk me through that entire process step-by-step.**

**Marcus:** Okay, so this was probably two weekends ago. Sunday morning I was feeling motivated — that's always when it happens. I opened my fridge, saw mostly condiments and half a block of tofu I couldn't remember buying, and decided I needed to actually plan the week. I Googled "high protein meal prep ideas," went down a rabbit hole for maybe 40 minutes looking at recipes, bookmarked like six of them, and then did absolutely none of those. I ended up just deciding on chicken thighs and rice because I knew how to make it.

Then I went to the grocery store — I usually go in-person to Trader Joe's because it's a 10-minute walk and I like it more than waiting for delivery. But I didn't write a proper list. I had a mental note of "chicken, rice, vegetables, something for breakfast." I wandered for 40 minutes, impulse-bought a bunch of stuff I thought I'd use, and came home with three things I didn't need and forgot garlic.

That afternoon I cooked the chicken and rice, felt great about myself, put it in containers. By Wednesday I was sick of it and ordered Thai food. Thursday I told myself I'd cook the remaining ingredients I'd bought — some zucchini and cherry tomatoes — but I worked late and they went mushy in the fridge. Classic.

For tools: I've tried a few apps. I downloaded Mealime and Paprika at different points. I don't really use them anymore. Mostly I'm just bouncing between Google, a Notes app on my phone with random grocery items I add throughout the week, and whatever mood I'm in on Sunday. It's not a system, it's more like organized chaos that usually degrades into takeout by Thursday.

---

**Q3: What is the most frustrating part — from planning through to cooking? Where's the biggest bottleneck?**

**Marcus:** Deciding what to eat. By a mile. The actual cooking part, once I've decided, is fine — I can follow a recipe. The shopping is annoying but manageable. But that initial decision-making phase — what do I actually want, that I'll still want on Thursday, that uses the stuff I have, that fits the time I'll realistically have this week — that's where I lose 45 minutes every Sunday and still don't feel confident about the answer.

Recent disaster: about a month ago I bought a bunch of salmon because I saw it on sale and told myself I'd cook it Tuesday. Then a work thing ran late Tuesday, same Wednesday, and by Thursday that salmon was not usable. That's probably $15 in the trash. Apparently the average American wastes around $728 a year in food — I believe it, because I feel like I personally am contributing way more than my share to that number.

My workaround right now is just keeping the bar super low. I pick one thing I can batch cook and accept I'll supplement with takeout. The ideal scenario? Something where the planning basically happens for me — where I don't have to stare at a blank page every Sunday. I just want someone or something to say "here's what makes sense for your week" and I say yes or no. The execution, I can handle.

### Section 2: Chat Behaviors & Trust

---

**Q1: When do you choose to use chat to get something done, instead of an app or website?**

**Marcus:** Honestly, pretty constantly now. I use Claude and ChatGPT every day at work — mostly for code-related stuff, but also just to think out loud. Writing a Jira ticket, summarizing a PR, explaining a weird error message. I think the statistic is something like 84% of developers use AI tools now, and for me it's not even a statistic anymore, it's just like... it's in my workflow the same way StackOverflow used to be.

I use chat when I want a back-and-forth or when the thing I need is hard to Google precisely. Like, "what should I eat for dinner given I have chicken breast, an onion, and 20 minutes" is a bad search query. It's a great chat prompt. I used exactly that last week and got a usable answer in 30 seconds.

What makes chat faster: I don't have to mentally translate what I want into a search query. I can be vague and sloppy with language and it still works. And I can push back — "okay but I don't have soy sauce, what else?" — and it just rolls with it.

What I avoid in chat: anything with real-time data I'd want to verify (like pricing, reviews, whether a restaurant is open). And anything where I want a grid-style comparison — chat is weirdly bad at presenting tables. I'd go to a website for that.

---

**Q2: What would you need to trust a chat assistant to manage something important for you?**

**Marcus:** This is a real question for me because I already trust it a lot for work stuff — but "important" is doing heavy lifting in that sentence. I trust it to suggest code, but I always review the diff. I trust it to draft an email, but I always read it before I send. The pattern is: I trust it to propose, but I control what gets committed.

For something like meals — which touches my health, my grocery spend, my actual time — I'd want the same dynamic. Show me what you're planning to do before you do it. Don't just add things to my cart or calendar without me seeing. And be consistent. If it gives me a good plan three weeks in a row and I verify it each time and it's right, that trust builds.

Mistakes I can tolerate: suggesting a recipe I end up not liking, or slightly misjudging how long something takes. That's fine. Mistakes I can't: adding something to my shopping cart I'm allergic to (even though I don't have allergies right now, the principle matters), or making assumptions about my dietary needs I haven't stated. The trust breakdown comes from overconfidence — acting like it knows things it doesn't.

Dealbreaker: if it got confidently wrong and I had to debug its reasoning. I deal with that enough in code. I don't want to do it for dinner.

### Section 3: Concept Reaction

---

**Q1: Imagine you could manage meals by chatting with an assistant connected to a meal planning app. What do you think it would do?**

**Marcus:** My first instinct is: I'd use it like a smarter version of what I already do — I'd just describe my week and ask it to figure out the plan. "I've got a busy week, I want to eat healthy, I have about 30 minutes to cook each night, here's what's in my fridge" — and it'd give me a weekly plan, a shopping list, and maybe a rough schedule of what to prep when.

The most useful part, to me, would be the initial planning — replacing that painful Sunday decision-making session. If it could cut that from 45 minutes of anxiety to a 3-minute conversation, that's a real win.

The part that sounds risky: if it's connected to a shopping cart and I forget to review before checkout. Or if it doesn't know I'm low on energy Thursday night and it scheduled something complex. It needs to understand context it can't see — my mood, my exhaustion level, whether I just had pizza at a work lunch. That stuff matters and I don't know how it handles gaps.

Who is this for? I think people like me — time-poor, not terrible at cooking but bad at planning, already comfortable talking to AI. People who've tried meal planning apps and dropped off. I don't think this replaces something for someone who actually loves planning meals — they've already got a system.

---

**Q2: Using your last planning week, what would you want to do via chat, and what would you not?**

**Marcus:** What I'd do via chat: the planning conversation. I'd start with something like "hey, I've got a normal week, I want to do some batch cooking Sunday, I'm trying to hit around 150g of protein a day, what should I make?" And I'd want it to ask me back: do I have any existing ingredients I want to use? How many nights am I actually cooking vs. eating out? Any repeats okay, or do I want variety?

Then I'd want it to output something concrete: a weekly plan (this meal Monday, this meal Tuesday, etc.), a deduplicated shopping list organized by category — like, produce together, protein together — and maybe a 10-minute "what to prep Sunday" summary.

What I'd not do via chat: searching for new recipes. That's better as a browsable visual experience — I want to see photos. And I wouldn't want to manage my pantry inventory in chat — keeping a running list of what I have via text sounds exhausting. That's a form problem, not a chat problem.

Where it breaks down in my real week: Wednesday. I have the plan, but then I get home at 8pm instead of 6:30pm and I'm not cooking what I said I'd cook. If the assistant can't adapt — "it's 8pm, you're tired, here's a 15-minute version of what you planned" — it's just going to make me feel bad about not following the plan, which is worse than having no plan.

---

**Q3: What data would you be comfortable sharing, and what's off-limits?**

**Marcus:** Comfortable: dietary preferences, rough schedule ("I work late Tuesdays"), ingredients on hand, rough weekly budget for food, how many nights I want to cook. That's all stuff I'd fill out in any app setup.

Less comfortable but potentially okay with it: location, because that could let it know which stores are near me. But I'd want to explicitly agree to that, not have it inferred.

Off-limits: actual financial data — like connecting to my bank account to see what I spent on DoorDash. I know that would theoretically help it calibrate, but no. And I wouldn't want it to infer my physical or medical stats without me telling it directly.

Review before save: I'd want to see the full plan before anything gets committed anywhere — before it adds to a shopping list, before it saves a calendar event. I want one "does this look right?" moment per session. If it's just silently writing to a database somewhere, I'll get nervous and stop using it.

Permissions that feel reasonable: read-only access to my preferences and past meals. Permissions that feel like too much: push notifications, calendar write without confirmation, location tracking. What would make me feel confident: a clear "here's what I'm storing and here's how to delete it" screen. That's it. It's not complicated, but most apps skip it and it erodes trust.

---

**Q4: What would make meal management via chat feel complete — not just a one-off suggestion?**

**Marcus:** The one-off thing is actually my worry. I've had that experience with apps — great first week, and then it doesn't know anything changed and it just keeps suggesting the same plan indefinitely. For this to feel like a system rather than a gimmick, it needs a few things.

First: it needs memory that's actually useful. Not "you like chicken" — I mean, it should remember that I over-bought salmon and wasted it, so it doesn't suggest three servings of salmon the next week. It should notice patterns.

Second: it needs to handle mid-week changes gracefully. If Monday's dinner took 45 minutes instead of 20, I want to be able to say that and have it recalibrate Tuesday and Wednesday rather than me having to re-plan from scratch.

Third: undo. I need to be able to back out of a plan easily. If I say yes to a plan and then decide on Sunday I actually just want to order pizza all week, I want to clear it without it being a whole thing.

Fourth: week-over-week improvement. After a month, it should be noticeably better than week one because it's learned my actual behavior, not just my stated preferences. Those are usually very different.

### Section 4: Wrap-up

---

**Q1: If you tried this, what would success look like after a week?**

**Marcus:** Concretely: I want the planning phase to take less than five minutes. That's the number I keep coming back to — if Sunday goes from a 45-minute stress spiral to a five-minute conversation, that's success. I'd notice it as "I didn't feel anxious about food this week." And I'd want to have wasted less food than usual — the salmon situation wouldn't have happened because the plan would have been realistic.

A clear sign it saved me time: I'd make it to Thursday without defaulting to delivery. Right now Thursday is the night everything falls apart. If Thursday holds, the system is working.

What would make me recommend it: if I could show a friend the conversation I had to set up the week — "look, three messages and I have a plan and a shopping list" — and it looked as simple as it should be. Nobody wants a tool that's clever but complicated. The recommendation pitch has to be "this is genuinely easier than what you do now."

---

**Q2: What's the minimum it needs to do for you to try it, and what's a dealbreaker?**

**Marcus:** Minimum: give me a weekly meal plan and a shopping list from a conversation. That's it. If those two things come out of a chat exchange and they're decent, I will try it. I don't need it to connect to Instacart on day one. I don't need the fridge integration or the calorie tracking. Those can come later. Start simple.

Setup effort I'd accept: a 5-minute onboarding conversation where I tell it my preferences, my typical schedule, any dietary things. A form I fill out once. That's reasonable. What's too much: connecting five different accounts, importing my grocery history, doing a pantry audit by photographing everything in my fridge. That's a product that doesn't respect my time.

If my top concern — the planning anxiety — were addressed? Yeah, I'd try it. Easily. The bar is not that high. I just need it to not feel like more work than the problem it's solving. That's the trap most of these apps fall into. They make planning more structured, but no less effortful. If chat can strip out the friction, I'm in.

## Key Insights Summary

- **The bottleneck is decision-making, not execution.** Marcus can cook — he just can't reliably decide what to cook in a way that holds up across a variable week. An AI that compresses Sunday planning from 45 minutes to 5 minutes addresses his most acute pain point.
- **Trust is built on the propose-then-confirm pattern.** As an AI-native developer, Marcus already has a mental model for working with AI: it proposes, he reviews, then it executes. Any meal planning tool that skips the review step will lose him.
- **Mid-week adaptability is the make-or-break feature.** Static weekly plans consistently fail Marcus by Thursday. The tool that wins isn't the one with the best plan on Sunday — it's the one that recovers gracefully when the plan meets real life.
- **Data boundaries are clear and non-negotiable.** He'll share preferences and schedule context willingly; financial data and passive location tracking are off-limits. Explicit consent at each data-sharing step is the minimum bar for sustained use.
- **He is a strong early adopter candidate but a flight risk without improvement loops.** He's tried meal planning apps before and abandoned them. What's different about chat is the lower friction for re-engagement — but if the tool doesn't get demonstrably smarter over time, he'll drift back to DoorDash within a month.
