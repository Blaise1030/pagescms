# Interview: Jasmine Torres — Budget-Conscious College Student

## Persona Background

Jasmine Torres, 22, is a final-year college student living with two roommates in a shared apartment near campus. She runs on a tight grocery budget of roughly $150–200/month and uses AI tools daily for schoolwork and life admin. Her meal habits are erratic — she'll batch-cook rice and beans one Sunday, then live on instant noodles and dining-hall coffee the following week. She wants to eat better but finds the cognitive load of budget meal planning genuinely overwhelming on top of coursework, a part-time job, and a chaotic schedule.

## Interview Transcript

### Section 1: Current Meal Planning Workflows

---

**Q1: Tell me about who you usually cook for and how your week tends to look. How predictable is your schedule week to week? Any dietary needs or strong preferences that affect meals?**

Okay so — it's me and my two roommates, but we basically operate as three completely separate kitchens in one apartment. Like, we'll share condiments and occasionally eat together, but there's no coordinated cooking happening. I'm mostly cooking for myself, which honestly makes it harder because nothing feels worth the effort for one person.

My schedule is all over the place. I've got classes Tuesday and Thursday, then I work at the campus coffee shop Wednesday and Friday mornings. So some nights I'm home by 4pm, some nights I don't get back until 9. I genuinely cannot tell you what next week looks like right now.

No dietary restrictions, but I try to eat less meat when I can because it's expensive and I've read the environmental stuff. I'm not strict about it though. If chicken thighs are on sale, I'm buying chicken thighs.

The thing people don't get is I'm spending like $150–175 a month on groceries. I know the average student supposedly spends over $260 a month on groceries — I've seen those numbers — but that's genuinely not an option for me right now. I have a part-time job, I get a small financial aid disbursement, and rent takes most of it. So every grocery trip is a math problem.

---

**Q2: Think about the last time you planned, shopped for, and cooked meals. Walk me through that entire process step-by-step.**

Okay, so last week I actually tried to do a real shop. I looked at what was in the fridge — basically nothing, just a sad bag of shredded cheese and some soy sauce — and I opened the notes app on my phone to make a list. Then I got distracted reading a TikTok about this high-protein rice bowl thing, so I pulled up that recipe, then went down a rabbit hole of like six other recipes, and then I had this massive list that would've cost me $80 minimum. So I scrapped it and just bought whatever felt cheap.

I went to the grocery store in person — I don't do delivery because the fees are insane on my budget. I spent maybe 25 minutes walking around, bought a big bag of rice, some canned black beans, a bunch of bananas, eggs, frozen broccoli, and a pack of ramen for emergencies. Total was like $38.

That Sunday I cooked a pot of rice and boiled some eggs. That was my "meal prep." By Tuesday I was bored of it. Wednesday night I was so tired after my shift that I just ate instant noodles for dinner. Thursday I skipped lunch entirely because I didn't have anything portable and didn't want to spend $12 at the student union.

For tools — it's basically just the notes app and my brain. I've tried meal planning apps twice. Both times I used them for like one week and then stopped because they kept suggesting ingredients I didn't have and didn't feel like buying. I do use ChatGPT sometimes to ask like "what can I make with rice, eggs, and frozen broccoli" and that's actually helpful.

---

**Q3: What is the most frustrating part of managing the entire cycle — from planning to grocery shopping, prepping, and cooking?**

Honestly? Starting. The deciding part. Like, the actual cooking is fine, I don't mind standing in the kitchen. But sitting down and figuring out what to make, checking what I have, building a list that fits my budget, making sure I'm not buying stuff I'll waste — that's the part that just doesn't happen. I'll literally choose instant noodles over spending 20 minutes doing that mental work.

The biggest bottleneck is the decision fatigue at the planning stage. By the time I've been in class or at work, I have zero executive function left for "what should I eat this week."

The worst recent thing: I bought a bunch of spinach because I was feeling optimistic. Used maybe a third of it. The rest went slimy in my fridge within like four days. Wasted almost $4 right there. And I know that sounds small but on my budget that's real money. And I'm not alone in this — apparently the average college student wastes like 110 pounds of food a year. I believe it.

My workaround right now is keeping a small set of "base" ingredients I always have — rice, canned beans, eggs, soy sauce, garlic — and just rotating what I do with them. It's boring but it prevents waste. An ideal workflow would be: I describe what I have and what my budget is, and something just tells me what to make with no wasted ingredients and no cognitive overhead. Basically zero friction from my end.

### Section 2: Chat Behaviors & Trust

---

**Q1: When do you choose to use chat to get something done, instead of an app or website?**

When I'm too lazy to navigate. Seriously — if I have to open an app, go to a search bar, parse a bunch of results, click through things... I'll just type a question in ChatGPT or Claude. It's faster for anything where I just need an answer, not a browsing experience.

Recent task: I asked Claude to help me rewrite a paragraph in a paper that was getting flagged by my prof for being unclear. Took 30 seconds. I've also used it to ask things like "what's the cheapest way to get enough protein if I only have $20 left this week."

What makes chat faster: I can be sloppy with my input and still get a useful output. I don't have to know what I'm looking for in advance. I can say "I have rice and eggs and I'm tired, what should I eat" and get something back immediately.

What I avoid doing through chat: anything that involves booking or buying something, because I want to see exactly what I'm clicking on. And anything time-sensitive where I need real-time data — like if a store is actually open, or whether a sale is still happening.

---

**Q2: What would you need to trust a chat assistant to manage something important for you?**

It would need to show its work. Don't just tell me "here's your meal plan." Tell me why — like, "this uses up the spinach you said you have before it goes bad" or "this comes in under your $40 budget." When I can see the reasoning, I can catch mistakes.

I check it by actually looking at the output and asking myself if it makes sense. If it suggests a recipe that calls for something I didn't say I had, that's a red flag. I've had ChatGPT hallucinate ingredients confidently — like, "just use the tahini in your pantry" and I'm like, I never said I had tahini.

Acceptable mistakes: recommending something I end up not feeling like eating. That happens with humans too. Or getting a quantity slightly wrong — like saying "2 cups of rice" when I'd normally do 1.5.

Unacceptable: telling me something is cheap when it's not, or generating a grocery list that's way over my budget without flagging it. Also if it doesn't know my dietary preferences and keeps suggesting stuff I said I don't like.

What would make me stop using it: if it felt like it was wasting my time more than saving it. Like if I have to correct it constantly, or if the output needs so much editing that I might as well have done it myself.

### Section 3: Concept Reaction

---

**Q1: Imagine you could manage meals by chatting with an assistant connected to a meal planning app. What do you think it would do? What sounds useful? What sounds risky?**

My first instinct is: I'd ask it "what should I eat this week for under $40" and it would generate a plan, pull in recipes, and maybe even auto-populate a grocery list. That's basically what I'd want.

Most useful part: the grocery list generation synced to a realistic budget. That is the exact thing I'm bad at. If it could take my budget, what I already have, and spit out a list that doesn't include things I'll waste — that would genuinely change my week.

Unclear or risky: if it's connected to a meal planning app, is it saving stuff automatically? Like, I don't want it to start ordering groceries or adding calendar events without me saying yes. And I'd be nervous about it not knowing my budget is actually tight — like, I don't want "budget-friendly" suggestions that are budget-friendly for someone who spends $300 a month, not $150.

Who this is best for: honestly, people like me. Busy, tight budget, decision fatigue, already comfortable with AI tools. 65% of students use generative AI weekly at this point — this is not a fringe use case anymore. The audience is there.

---

**Q2: Using your last planning week, what would you want to do via chat, and what would you not?**

I would absolutely want to do the planning conversation through chat. Like: "I have rice, eggs, frozen broccoli, canned black beans, garlic, soy sauce. My budget for the week is $35. I work late Wednesday and Friday. What should I eat?" And have it give me a plan that accounts for those late nights with something easy.

I would NOT want to do the actual grocery shopping through chat — I want to walk the store myself and see what's on sale that wasn't on the list. Deals change everything and I need to see it with my own eyes.

First thing I'd type: probably exactly what I said above. Very literal, very specific.

What I'd want it to ask back: "Do you have any other ingredients?" and "Do you prefer anything in particular this week?" Not a 10-question onboarding form — just 1 or 2 clarifying questions.

Output I'd want: a 5–7 day plan laid out simply (breakfast/lunch/dinner or just dinner if it knows I'm skipping lunch a lot), a consolidated grocery list with estimated cost, and maybe a note on which nights are fastest to make.

Where it would break down: if my Wednesday plans change and now I'm home early. The plan gets thrown off and suddenly I don't know if I should follow it or freestyle. Also if I impulse-buy something at the store that wasn't on the list, the whole plan feels out of date.

---

**Q3: What data would you be comfortable sharing for this to work, and what would be off-limits?**

Comfortable sharing: what ingredients I have, my weekly budget, my rough schedule in terms of busy vs. easy nights, dietary preferences. That's all it needs, honestly.

Off-limits: my financial account details, my location beyond just city/general area for store recommendations, and anything that would get shared with third parties for ads. I'm already paranoid about that.

What I'd want to review before saving: everything. If it generates a meal plan, I want to see it and say "yes, save this" before it touches the app. Same with a grocery list — don't auto-add anything. Show me first.

Reasonable permissions: read my pantry list if I've entered one, save my preferences like "she doesn't like fish," generate and suggest plans. That's it.

Too much: location tracking, access to my calendar in a way that feels invasive, or anything that involves payment info. And definitely no posting to social or syncing with things I didn't explicitly connect.

What would make me feel confident: a clear plain-language summary of what data it's using and why. Not a 40-page terms of service. Just: "Here's what I know about you, here's what I'm doing with it." That's it.

---

**Q4: What would make meal management via chat feel complete, not just a one-off suggestion?**

Memory. That's the big one. If I have to re-explain my budget and my pantry every single conversation, I'll stop using it within a week. It needs to remember "Jasmine is on $150/month, doesn't like fish, always has rice and beans in stock." And it should be able to update that — like if I say "I'm out of eggs" it should note that.

Review and undo: I want to be able to say "actually, swap Thursday's dinner for something faster" and have it update just that. Not regenerate everything. Fine-grained edits matter.

Mid-week changes: this is huge. My life doesn't follow a plan. If I end up eating out with friends on Tuesday, I need to be able to tell it "skip Tuesday" and have it redistribute or just note the leftovers. Rigid plans that can't flex are useless for my actual life.

What I'd want it to remember over time: my staple ingredients, which meals I said I liked vs. didn't, my average budget, and the patterns in my schedule. Basically, it should start to feel like it knows me a little — like a friend who knows "Jasmine is not cooking a complex meal on a Friday night, don't even suggest it."

### Section 4: Wrap-up

---

**Q1: If you tried this, what would success look like after a week of using it?**

Success is: I skipped fewer meals and wasted less food. Those are my two core problems right now. If I can look at the end of the week and say "I actually ate real food most days and I didn't throw out anything," that's a win.

The earlier frustration it would need to address most: the planning decision fatigue. If I still have to do all the mental work of figuring out what to eat, the tool has failed. The whole point is that it absorbs that cognitive load.

Clear sign it saved me time: if I go from spending 30–45 minutes bouncing between notes apps and recipe TikToks every Sunday to just sending one message and getting a plan in 2 minutes. That time savings is immediately obvious.

What would make me recommend it: if I could say "I ate better and spent less money this week." That's the pitch. I know I have friends who are in the same situation — 41% of students experienced food insecurity in the past month, so this is not a niche problem. If it worked for me, I'd be telling my roommates about it immediately.

---

**Q2: What's the minimum it would need to do for you to try it, and what would be a dealbreaker?**

Minimum viable version: take my budget + what I have + a rough schedule, give me a week of dinners and a grocery list. That's it. Nothing fancy. If it does that well, I'm in.

Setup effort I'd tolerate: maybe 5 minutes to enter my pantry staples and my budget. That's fine. More than that and I'd probably bail. I don't have the patience for a 15-question onboarding survey.

Dealbreakers:
- Suggesting meals that go over my budget without flagging it
- Requiring me to re-enter my preferences every session (no memory)
- Any kind of auto-purchasing or automatic calendar blocking without my explicit approval
- If the recipes consistently call for specialty ingredients I'd have to hunt down

If my top concern — the budget accuracy — were addressed, yes, I'd try it. The gap between what meal planning apps think "budget-friendly" means and what it actually means for someone at $150/month is enormous. If this thing actually respects a real student budget, that alone is enough to get me through the door.

## Key Insights Summary

- **Decision fatigue is the core barrier, not cooking skill.** Jasmine can cook and is willing to prep — the blocker is the weekly cognitive load of figuring out what to make within a tight budget. Any solution must absorb this planning overhead, not just assist with it.
- **Budget accuracy is a trust prerequisite.** Generic "budget-friendly" suggestions calibrated for $300+/month budgets will immediately erode trust. The tool must respect genuinely constrained budgets (~$150/month) and be transparent when suggestions approach or exceed those limits.
- **Memory and mid-week flexibility are non-negotiable for stickiness.** A one-time plan generator won't retain users. Jasmine needs the assistant to remember her staples, preferences, and patterns, and to handle real-life deviations without requiring a full restart.
- **She is already AI-native and will adopt quickly — but will also quit quickly.** With 65% of students using gen AI weekly, the adoption barrier is low. But Jasmine has high churn instincts: if the tool adds friction rather than removing it, she drops it within a week.
- **Data sharing comfort is narrow and consent-driven.** She'll share pantry inventory, budget, and schedule details — but any perceived overreach (location tracking, payment access, auto-saving without review) is an immediate dealbreaker. Explicit control before any action is saved is essential.
